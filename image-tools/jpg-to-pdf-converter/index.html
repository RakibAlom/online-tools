How the JPG to PDF Conversion Works (Behind the Scenes)

At a high level, converting images to PDF means taking image data and placing it onto PDF pages. Each page has a physical size (A4/Letter/original size), and the image must be scaled and positioned correctly. This converter uses a client-side PDF engine to:

  1. Read each JPG file locally in your browser.
  2. Prepare a page in the PDF with your chosen size and orientation.

Page Size Options: A4, Letter, and Original Image Size

A4 (210×297mm)

A4 is the most common paper size worldwide. Choose A4 for school assignments, business documents, and general printing. When you export to A4, each JPG is scaled and centered on an A4 page according to your margin and fit settings.

Letter (8.5×11 in)

Letter is widely used in the United States. If your printer and local workflows use Letter, choosing it reduces print scaling inconsistencies.

Original Image Size (DPI-based)

This option creates PDF pages sized based on each image’s pixel dimensions and a DPI value. DPI controls how pixels translate into physical size. Higher DPI makes the page physically smaller; lower DPI makes it larger. “Original size” is useful for archiving images as pages and for workflows where page-to-image sizing must be consistent.

Orientation, Margin, and Fit Mode

Orientation determines whether a page is portrait or landscape. Auto mode selects the best orientation for each image based on its dimensions. If you want consistent pages, you can force portrait or landscape.

Margin controls whitespace around the content. Margins are important for printing and professional appearance. A 10mm margin is a balanced default; increase it for more whitespace, reduce it for larger content coverage.

Fit mode decides how the image is placed:

  • Contain (no cropping): Shows the entire image. Best for documents, receipts, and screenshots.
  • Cover (may crop edges): Fills the page area. Best for full-page photo layouts where cropping is acceptable.

Max Long Edge (px): The Practical Way to Reduce Huge PDFs

Modern phones can capture images that are 4000–12000 pixels on the long edge. Embedding such images into a PDF can create very large files and can slow down processing, particularly on mobile devices. Max Long Edge lets you resize images before embedding.

  • 0 px: No resizing (maximum detail).
  • 6000 px: High quality for most uses, smaller file, faster export.
  • 4000 px: Great balance for screen viewing and typical printing.
  • 2500–3500 px: Smaller PDFs for fast sharing and uploads.
